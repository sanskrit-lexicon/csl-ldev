ATHARVA¦(M). Among the Vedas, this has the fourth
<div n="lb"/>place. It comprises different kinds of incantations of
<div n="lb"/>occult powers for the destruction of enemies. Atharvan
<div n="lb"/>the son of Vasiṣṭha was the author of this Veda. In
<div n="lb"/>Chapter 6 of the third section of Viṣṇu Purāṇa the
<div n="lb"/>following reference is made to Atharvaveda: “Sumantu
<div n="lb"/>Maharṣi who was of infinite glory, first taught this
<div n="lb"/>Atharvaveda to his disciple Kabandha. (Sumantu
<div n="lb"/>Maharṣi was the son of Jaimini, who was the disciple
<div n="lb"/>of Vedavyāsa). Kabandha divided Atharvaveda into
<div n="lb"/>two parts and communicated them to two Maharṣis
<div n="lb"/>named Devadarśa and Pathya. Devadarśa's disciples
<div n="lb"/>were: Medhā, Brahmabali, Śautkāyani and Pippalāda.
<div n="lb"/>Pathya had three disciples named Jābāli, Kumudādi
<div n="lb"/>and Śaunaka. They also made Atharvaveda compila-
<div n="lb"/>tions. Śaunaka divided his compilation into two and
<div n="lb"/>gave one part to Babhru and the other to Saindhava.
<div n="lb"/>Muñjikeśa learnt it from Saindhava and divided the
<div n="lb"/>compilation first into two and later into three parts.
<div n="lb"/>The five divisions of the Atharvaveda—Nakṣatrakalpa,
<div n="lb"/>Vedakalpa, Saṃhitākalpa, Āṅgirasakalpa and Śānti-
<div n="lb"/>kalpa, were made by Muñjikeśa. Nakṣatrakalpa con-
<div n="lb"/>tains Brahma's works: Saṃhitākalpa contains Mantra
<div n="lb"/>Vidhi; Āngirasakalpa contains ābhicāra and Śāntikalpa
<div n="lb"/>contains taming of horses, elephants etc.
