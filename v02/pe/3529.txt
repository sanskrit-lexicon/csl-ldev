KHAṬVĀṄGA¦.
<div n="NI"/>1) {%General Information.%} A King of the Ikṣvāku dynasty,
<div n="lb"/>known by the name Dilīpa also. Bhāgavata Skandha 9,
<div n="lb"/>states that Khaṭvāṅga was the son of the grandson of
<div n="lb"/>Kalmāṣapāda. Aśmaka was the son of Kalmāṣapāda,
<div n="lb"/>Mūlaka the son of Aśmaka and Khaṭvāṅga the son of
<div n="lb"/>Mūlaka.
<div n="NI"/>Khaṭvāṅga who was a royal hermit once pleased God
<div n="lb"/>and understood how long he would live. From that day
<div n="lb"/>onwards he left all the work of administration in the
<div n="lb"/>hands of ministers and spent the remaining days in
<div n="lb"/>devotion and meditation. (Bhāgavata, Skandha 2).
<div n="NI"/>2) {%Particular details.%} In Mahābhārata it is mentioned
<div n="lb"/>that Khaṭvāṅga was the son of the woman called
<div n="lb"/>Ilibilā and that he had the name Dilīpa also. Khaṭ-
<div n="lb"/>vāṅga was one of the sixteen famous Kings of Bhārata.
<div n="lb"/>The sixteen Kings were Marutta, Suhotra, Paurava,
<div n="lb"/>Śibi, Śrī Rāma, Bhagīratha, Khaṭvāṅga, (Dilīpa) Mān-
<div n="lb"/>dhātā, Yayāti, Ambarīṣa, Śaśabindu, Gaya, Rantideva,
<div n="lb"/>Bharata, Pṛthu and Paraśurāma. Khaṭvāṅga performed
<div n="lb"/>one hundred sacrifices. At the time of sacrifice he made
<div n="lb"/>golden roads. Even Indra came to the sacrifice. The
<div n="lb"/>Devas blessed Khaṭvāṅga on that day. See under
<div n="lb"/>Dilīpa. (M.B. Droṇa Parva, Chapter 61).
