{@Ikṣvāku.@}¦ — In the Rigveda this name occurs but once,<sup>1</sup> and in
<div n="lb"/>a doubtful context. It is clear, however, that it denotes a
<div n="lb"/>prince; later interpretations make {@Asamāti,@} whose name is read
<div n="lb"/>into the hymn, an Ikṣvāku prince.<sup>2</sup> In the Atharvaveda<sup>3</sup> also
<div n="lb"/>the name is found in only one passage, where it is uncertain
<div n="lb"/>whether a descendant of Ikṣvāku, or Ikṣvāku himself, is referred
<div n="lb"/>to; in either case he seems to be regarded as an ancient hero.
<div n="lb"/>In the Pañcaviṃśa Brāhmaṇa<sup>4</sup> mention is made of {@Tryaruṇa@}
<div n="lb"/>{@Traidhātva@} Aikṣvāka, who is identical with the {@Tryaruṇa@}
<div n="lb"/>{@Traivṛṣṇa@} of the Bṛhaddevatā,<sup>5</sup> and with {@Tryaruṇa Trasa-@}
<div n="lb"/>{@dasyu@} in the Rigveda.<sup>6</sup> The connection of Trasadasyu with
<div n="lb"/>the Ikṣvākus is confirmed by the fact that {@Purukutsa@} was an
<div n="lb"/>Aikṣvāka, according to the Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa.<sup>7</sup> Thus the
<div n="lb"/>Ikṣvāku line was originally a line of princes of the Pūrus.
<div n="lb"/>Zimmer<sup>8</sup> places them on the upper Indus, but they may well
<div n="lb"/>have been somewhat further east.<sup>9</sup> Later Ikṣvāku is connected
<div n="lb"/>with Ayodhyā
