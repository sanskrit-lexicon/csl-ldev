{@Nāman,@}¦ ‘name,’ is a common word from the Rigveda
<div n="lb"/>onwards. The Gṛhya Sūtras<sup>1</sup> give elaborate rules for the
<div n="lb"/>formation of the names of children, but more important is
<div n="lb"/>the distinction between the secret ({%guhya%}) and the ordinary
<div n="lb"/>name, though the rules as to the secret name are not at all
<div n="lb"/>consistent. The secret name is already recognized in the
<div n="lb"/>Rigveda,<sup>2</sup> and is referred to in the Brāhmaṇas,<sup>3</sup> one secret
<div n="lb"/>name, that of Arjuna for Indra, being given in the Śatapatha
<div n="lb"/>Brāhmaṇa.<sup>4</sup> It is to be noted that the rule as to giving the
[Page1-444+ 54]
<div n="lb"/>designation of a Nakṣatra (lunar asterism) as the secret name
<div n="lb"/>or otherwise is not illustrated by a single recorded name of a
<div n="lb"/>teacher in the Brāhmaṇas.<sup>5</sup>
<div n="P"/>The Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa<sup>6</sup> several times mentions the
<div n="lb"/>adoption of a second name with a view to securing success,
<div n="lb"/>and also refers to the adoption of another name for purposes
<div n="lb"/>of distinction.<sup>7</sup>
<div n="P"/>In actual practice two names are usually found in the
<div n="lb"/>Brāhmaṇas, the second being a patronymic or a metronymic,
<div n="lb"/>as in {@Kakṣīvant Auśija@}<sup>8</sup> (if the story of the slave woman Uśij
<div n="lb"/>as his mother is correct), or {@Bṛhaduktha Vāmneya,@}<sup>9</sup> ‘son of
<div n="lb"/>Vāmnī,’ though the relationship may, of course, be not direct
<div n="lb"/>parentage, but more remote descent.<sup>10</sup> Three names are less
<div n="lb"/>common — for example, {@Kūśāmba Svāyava Lātavya,@}<sup>11</sup> ‘son of
<div n="lb"/>Svāyu, of the Lātavya (son of Latu) family,’ or {@Devataras@}
<div n="lb"/>{@Śyāvasāyana Kāśyapa,@}<sup>12</sup> where the patronymic and the Gotra
<div n="lb"/>name are both found. In other cases the names probably
<div n="lb"/>have a local reference — {%e.g.,%} {@Kauśāmbeya@} and {@Gāṅgya.@} Fre-
<div n="lb"/>quently the patronymic only is given, as {@Bhārgava, Maudgalya,@}
<div n="lb"/>etc., or two patronymics are used. The simple name is often
<div n="lb"/>used for the patronymic — {%e.g.,%} {@Trasadasyu.@}<sup>13</sup> In a few cases
<div n="lb"/>the name of the wife is formed from the husband's name,<sup>14</sup> as
