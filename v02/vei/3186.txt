<L>3186<pc>2-358<k1>शरीर<k2>शरीर
{@Śarīra,@}¦ ‘body,’ is a word of frequent occurrence in Vedic
<div n="lb"/>literature.<sup>1</sup> The interest of the Vedic Indians seems early to
<div n="lb"/>have been attracted to the consideration of questions connected
<div n="lb"/>with the anatomy of the body. Thus a hymn of the Atharva-
<div n="lb"/>veda<sup>2</sup> enumerates many parts of the body with some approach
<div n="lb"/>to accuracy and orderly arrangement.<sup>3</sup> It mentions the heels
<div n="lb"/>({%pārṣṇī%}), the flesh ({%māṃsa%}), the ankle-bones ({%gulphau%}), the
<div n="lb"/>fingers ({%aṅgulīḥ%}), the apertures ({%kha%}), the two metatarsi
<div n="lb"/>({%uchlakau%}), the tarsus ({%pratiṣṭhā%}), the two knee-caps ({%aṣṭhī-%}
<div n="lb"/>{%vantau%}), the two legs ({%jaṅghe%}), the two knee-joints ({%jānunoḥ%}
<div n="lb"/>{%sandhī%}). Then comes above the two kness ({%jānū%}) the four-
<div n="lb"/>sided ({%catuṣṭaya%}), pliant ({%śithira%}) trunk ({%kabandha%}). The two
<div n="lb"/>hips ({%śroṇī%}) and the two thighs ({%ārū%}) are the props of the frame
[Page2-359+ 61]
<div n="lb"/>({%kusindha%}). Next come the breast-bone ({%uras%}), the cervical
<div n="lb"/>cartilages ({%grīvāḥ%}), the two breast pieces ({%stanau%}), the two
<div n="lb"/>shoulder-blades ({%kaphoḍau%}), the neck-bones ({%skandhau%}), and the
<div n="lb"/>backbones ({%pṛṣṭīḥ%}), the collar-bones ({%aṃsau%}), the arms ({%bāhū%}),
<div n="lb"/>the seven apertures in the head ({%sapta khāni śīrṣaṇi%}), the ears
<div n="lb"/>({%karṇau%}), the nostrils ({%nāsike%}), the eyes ({%cakṣaṇī%}), the mouth
<div n="lb"/>({%mukha%}), the jaws ({%hanū%}), the tongue ({%jihvā%}), the brain ({%mas-%}
<div n="lb"/>{%tiṣka%}), the forehead ({%lalāṭa%}), the facial bone ({%kakāṭikā%}), the
<div n="lb"/>cranium ({%kapāla%}), and the structure of the jaws ({%cityā hanvoḥ%}).
<div n="P"/>This system presents marked similarities with the later
<div n="lb"/>system of Caraka and Suśruta,<sup>4</sup> which render certain the names
<div n="lb"/>ascribed to the several terms by Hoernle. {%Kaphoḍau,%} which is
<div n="lb"/>variously read in the manuscripts,<sup>5</sup> is rendered ‘collar-bone’ by
<div n="lb"/>Whitney, but ‘elbow’ in the St. Petersburg Dictionary.
<div n="lb"/>{%Skandha%} in the plural regularly<sup>6</sup> denotes ‘neck-bones,’ or, more
<div n="lb"/>precisely, ‘cervical vertebræ,’ a part denoted also by {%uṣṇihā%} in
<div n="lb"/>the plural.<sup>7</sup> {%Pṛṣṭī%}<sup>8</sup> denotes not ‘rib,’ which is {%parśu,%}<sup>9</sup> but a
<div n="lb"/>transverse process of a vertebra, and so the vertebra itself,
<div n="lb"/>there being in the truncal portion of the spinal column seventeen
<div n="lb"/>vertebræ and thirty-four transverse processes. The vertebræ
<div n="lb"/>are also denoted by {%kīkasā%} in the plural,<sup>10</sup> which sometimes<sup>11</sup> is
<div n="lb"/>limited to the upper portion of the vertebral column, sometimes<sup>12</sup>
<div n="lb"/>to the thoracic portion of the spine. {%Anūka%} also denotes the
<div n="lb"/>vertebral column,<sup>13</sup> or more specially the lumbar<sup>14</sup> or thoracic<sup>15</sup>
<div n="lb"/>portion of the spine; it is said in the Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa<sup>15</sup>
[Page2-360+ 55]
<div n="lb"/>that there are twenty transverse processes in the lumbar spine
<div n="lb"/>({%udara%}) and thirty-two in the thoracic, which gives twenty-six
<div n="lb"/>vertebræ, the true number (but the modern division is seven
<div n="lb"/>cervical, twelve thoracic, five lumbar, and two false — the
<div n="lb"/>sacrum and the coccyx). The vertebral column is also denoted
<div n="lb"/>by {%karūkara,%}<sup>16</sup> which, however, is usually found in the plural<sup>17</sup>
<div n="lb"/>denoting the transverse processes of the vertebræ, a sense
<div n="lb"/>expressed also by {%kuntāpa.%}<sup>18</sup>
<div n="P"/>{%Grīvā,%} in the plural, denotes cervical vertebræ, the number
<div n="lb"/>seven being given by the Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa,<sup>19</sup> but usually<sup>20</sup>
<div n="lb"/>the word simply means windpipe, or, more accurately, the
<div n="lb"/>cartilaginous rings under the skin. {@Jatru,@} also in the plural,
<div n="lb"/>denotes the cervical cartilages,<sup>21</sup> or possibly the costal carti-
<div n="lb"/>lages, which are certainly so called in the Śatapatha Brāh-
<div n="lb"/>maṇa,<sup>22</sup> where their number is given as eight.
<div n="P"/>{%Bhaṃsas,%} which occurs thrice in the Atharvaveda,<sup>23</sup> seems to
<div n="lb"/>denote the pubic bone or arch rather than the ‘buttocks’ or
<div n="lb"/>‘fundament,’ as Whitney<sup>24</sup> takes it.
<div n="P"/>In the Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa<sup>25</sup> the number of bones in the
<div n="lb"/>the human body is given as 360. The number of the bones
<div n="lb"/>of the head and trunk are given in another passage<sup>26</sup> as
<div n="lb"/>follows: The head is threefold, consisting of skin ({%tvac%}), bone
<div n="lb"/>({%asthi%}), brain ({%matiṣka%}); the neck has 15 bones: 14 transverse
<div n="lb"/>processes ({%karūkara%}) and the strength ({%vīrya%}) — {%i.e.,%} the bone of
<div n="lb"/>the centre regarded as one — as the 15th; the breast has 17:
<div n="lb"/>16 cervical cartilages ({%jatru%}), and the sternum ({%uras%}) as the
<div n="lb"/>17th; the abdominal portion of the spine has 21: 20 trans-
[Page2-361+ 45]
<div n="lb"/>verse processes ({%kuntāpa%}), and the abdominal portion ({%udara%}) as
<div n="lb"/>the 21st; the two sides have 27: 26 ribs ({%parśu%}), and the two
<div n="lb"/>sides as the 27th; the thoracic portion of the spine ({%anūka%})
<div n="lb"/>has 33: 32 transverse processes, and the thoracic portion
<div n="lb"/>as 33rd.
<div n="P"/>There are several enumerations of the parts of the body, not
<div n="lb"/>merely of the skeleton, in the Yajurveda Saṃhitās.<sup>27</sup> They
<div n="lb"/>include the hair ({%lomāni%}), skin ({%tvac%}), flesh ({%māṃsa%}), bone
<div n="lb"/>({%asthi%}), marrow ({%majjan%}), liver ({%yakṛt%}), lungs ({%kloman%}), kidneys
<div n="lb"/>({%matasne%}), gall ({%pitta%}), entrails ({%āntrāṇi%}), bowels ({%gudāḥ%}), spleen
<div n="lb"/>({%plīhan%}), navel ({%nābhi%}), belly ({%udara%}), rectum ({%vaniṣṭhu%}), womb
<div n="lb"/>({%yoni%}), penis ({%plāśi%} and {%śepa%}), face ({%mukha%}), head ({%śiras%}),
<div n="lb"/>tongue ({%jihvā%}), mouth ({%āsan%}), rump ({%pāyu%}), leech ({%vāla%}), eye
<div n="lb"/>({%cakṣus%}), eyelashes ({%pakṣmāṇi%}), eyebrows ({%utāni%}), nose ({%nas%}), breath
<div n="lb"/>({%vyāna%}), nose-hairs ({%nasyāni%}), ears ({%karṇau%}), brows ({%bhrū%}), body
<div n="lb"/>or trunk ({%ātman%}), waist ({%upastha%}), hair on the face ({%śmaśrūṇi%}),
<div n="lb"/>and on the head ({%keśāḥ%}). Another enumeration<sup>28</sup> gives {%śiras,%}
<div n="lb"/>{%mukha, keśāḥ, śmaśrūṇi, prāṇa%} (breath), {%cakṣus, śrotra%} (ear), {%jihvā,%}
<div n="lb"/>{%vāc%} (speech), {%manas%} (mind), {%aṅgulīḥ, aṅgāni%} (limbs), {%bāhū, hastau%}
<div n="lb"/>(hands), {%karṇau, ātmā, uras%} (sternum), {%pṛṣṭīḥ%} (vertebræ), {%udara,%}
<div n="lb"/>{%aṃsau, grīvāḥ, śroṇī, ūrū, aratnī%} (elbows), {%jānūni, nābhi, pāyu,%}
<div n="lb"/>{%bhasat%} (fundament), {%āṇḍau%} (testicles), {%pasas%} (membrum virile),
<div n="lb"/>{%jaṅghā, pad%} (foot), {%lomāni, tvac, māṃsa, asthi, majjan.%} Another
<div n="lb"/>set of names<sup>29</sup> includes {%vaniṣṭhu, purītat%} (pericardium), {%lomāni,%}
<div n="lb"/>{%tvac, lohita%} (blood), {%medas%} (fat), {%māṃsāni, snāvāni%} (sinews),
<div n="lb"/>{%asthīni, majjānaḥ, retas%} (semen), {%pāyu, kośya%} (flesh near the
<div n="lb"/>heart), {%pārśvya%} (intercostal flesh), etc.
<div n="P"/>The bones of the skeleton of the horse are enumerated in the
<div n="lb"/>Yajurveda Saṃhitās.<sup>30</sup>
<div n="P"/>In the Aitareya Āraṇyaka<sup>31</sup> the human body is regarded as
<div n="lb"/>made up of one hundred and one items; there are four parts,
[Page2-362+ 61]
<div n="lb"/>each of twenty-five members, with the trunk as one hundred
<div n="lb"/>and first. In the two upper parts there are five four-jointed<sup>32</sup>
<div n="lb"/>fingers, two {%kakṣasī%} (of uncertain meaning),<sup>33</sup> the arm ({%dos%}), the
<div n="lb"/>collar-bone ({%akṣa%}), and the shoulder-blade ({%aṃsa-phalaka%}). In
<div n="lb"/>the two lower portions there are five four-jointed toes, the
<div n="lb"/>thigh, the leg, and three articulations, according to Sāyaṇa's
<div n="lb"/>commentary.
<div n="P"/>The Śāṅkhāyana Āraṇyaka<sup>34</sup> enumerates three bones in the
<div n="lb"/>head,<sup>35</sup> three joints ({%parvāṇi%}) in the neck,<sup>36</sup> the collar-bone
<div n="lb"/>({%akṣa%}),<sup>37</sup> three joints in the fingers,<sup>38</sup> and twenty-one transverse
<div n="lb"/>processes in the spine ({%anūka%}).<sup>39</sup> The Maitrāyaṇī Saṃhitā<sup>40</sup>
<div n="lb"/>enumerates four constituents in the head ({%prāṇa, cakṣus, śrotra,%}
<div n="lb"/>{%vāc%}), but there are many variations, the number going up to
<div n="lb"/>twelve on one calculation.<sup>41</sup> In the Taittirīya Upaniṣad<sup>42</sup> an
<div n="lb"/>enumeration is given consisting of {%carma%} (skin), {%māṃsa, snāvan,%}
<div n="lb"/>{%asthi,%} and {%majjan;%} and the Aitareya Brāhmaṇa<sup>43</sup> has {%lomāni, māṃsa,%}
<div n="lb"/>{%tvac, asthi, majjan,%} and the Aitareya Āraṇyaka<sup>44</sup> couples {%majjānaḥ,%}
<div n="lb"/>{%snāvāni,%} and {%asthīni.%} Other terms relating to the body are
<div n="lb"/>{%kaṅkūṣa,%}<sup>45</sup> perhaps a part of the ear,<sup>46</sup> {%yoni%} (female organ),
<div n="lb"/>{%kakṣa%}<sup>47</sup> (armpit), {@Danta@} (tooth), {%nakha%} (nail), {%prapada%}<sup>48</sup> (forepart
<div n="lb"/>of the foot), {%halīkṣṇa%}<sup>49</sup> (gall).
