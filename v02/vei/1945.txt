{@Paiṅgya,@}¦ ‘descendant of Piṅga,’ is the name of a teacher
<div n="lb"/>who is repeatedly mentioned as an authority in the Kauṣītaki
<div n="lb"/>Brāhmaṇa,<sup>1</sup> where<sup>2</sup> also his doctrine is called the Paiṅgya.
<div n="lb"/>This teacher is further referred to in the Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa,<sup>3</sup>
<div n="lb"/>which also speaks of {@Madhuka@} Paiṅgya.<sup>4</sup> It is, of course,
<div n="lb"/>impossible to say whether there was only one Paiṅgya or several
<div n="lb"/>Paiṅgyas. The followers of Paiṅgya are called Paiṅgins in the
<div n="lb"/>Nidāna<sup>5</sup> and Anupada<sup>6</sup> Sūtras. His text-book is called Paiṅga
<div n="lb"/>in the Anupada Sūtra,<sup>7</sup> while the Āpastamba Śrauta Sūtra<sup>8</sup>
<div n="lb"/>mentions a Paiṅgāyani Brāhmaṇa. It is clear that Paiṅgya
<div n="lb"/>was a teacher of a Rigveda school allied to the Kauṣītakis.
<div n="lb"/>Paiṅgi is a patronymic of {@Yāska@} in the Anukramanī of the
<div n="lb"/>Ātreyī Śākhā.<sup>9</sup>
