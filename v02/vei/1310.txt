{@Didhiṣu@}¦ in the Rigveda denotes a ‘wooer.’ It is applied<sup>1</sup>
<div n="lb"/>to the relative, probably brother-in-law,<sup>2</sup> who takes the place
<div n="lb"/>of the husband at the funeral rite, and who, as in the Hebraic
<div n="lb"/>levirate, is to beget a child by the brother's wife if there is
<div n="lb"/>no son.<sup>3</sup> Hillebrandt<sup>4</sup> and Lanman<sup>5</sup> consider that the word
<div n="lb"/>originally meant only ‘wooer,’ and applied to the king who,
<div n="lb"/>after the chief queen had lain beside the dead victim in the
<div n="lb"/>Puruṣamedha or ‘human sacrifice,’ claimed her again; but this
<div n="lb"/>view is hardly plausible.<sup>6</sup> The term is also applied to the god
<div n="lb"/>Pūṣan<sup>7</sup> as the wooer of his mother, apparently Sūryā.<sup>8</sup>
<F>1) x. 18, 18 = Av. xviii. 3, 2 (where
<div n="lb"/>{%didhiṣus%} is merely a bad reading) =
<div n="lb"/>Taittirīya Āraṇyaka, vi. 1, 3.</F>
<F>2) Āśvalāyana Śrauta Sūtra, iv. 2, 18,

<div n="lb"/>({%devṛ%}), a representative of the husband
<div n="lb"/>(there is nothing to show whether he
<div n="lb"/>is identical with the preceding or not),
<div n="lb"/>a pupil, or an aged servant ({%jarad-dāsa%}).</F>
