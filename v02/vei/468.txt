{@Opaśa@}¦ is a word of somewhat doubtful sense, occurring in
<div n="lb"/>the Rigveda,<sup>1</sup> the Atharvaveda,<sup>2</sup> and occasionally later.<sup>3</sup> It
<div n="lb"/>probably means a ‘plait’ as used in dressing the hair, especially
<div n="lb"/>of women,<sup>4</sup> but apparently, in earlier times,<sup>5</sup> of men also.
<div n="lb"/>The goddess Sinīvālī is called {%svaupaśā,%}<sup>6</sup> an epithet of doubtful
<div n="lb"/>sense, from which Zimmer<sup>7</sup> conjectures that the wearing of
[Page1-125+ 50]
<div n="lb"/>false plaits of hair was not unknown in Vedic times. What
<div n="lb"/>was the difference between the braids referred to in the
<div n="lb"/>epithets {%pṛthu ṣṭuka,%}<sup>8</sup> ‘having broad braids,’ and {%viṣita-ṣṭuka,%}<sup>9</sup>
<div n="lb"/>‘having loosened braids,’ and the Opaśa cannot be made out
<div n="lb"/>from the evidence available. Geldner<sup>10</sup> thinks that the original
<div n="lb"/>sense was ‘horn,’ and that when the word applies to Indra<sup>11</sup>
<div n="lb"/>it means ‘diadem.’
