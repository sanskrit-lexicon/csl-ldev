{@Gotra@}¦ occurs several times in the Rigveda<sup>1</sup> in the account of
<div n="lb"/>the mythic exploits of Indra. Roth<sup>2</sup> interprets the word as
<div n="lb"/>‘cowstall,’ while Geldner<sup>3</sup> thinks ‘herd’ is meant. The latter
<div n="lb"/>sense seems to explain best the employment which the term
<div n="lb"/>shows in the later literature as denoting the ‘family’ or ‘clan,’
<div n="lb"/>and which is found in the Chāndogya Upaniṣad.<sup>4</sup>
<div n="P"/>In the Gṛhya Sūtras<sup>5</sup> stress is laid on the prohibition of
[Page1-236+ 55]
<div n="lb"/>marriage within a Gotra, or with a Sapiṇḍa of the mother of
<div n="lb"/>the bridegroom — that is to say, roughly, with agnates and
<div n="lb"/>cognates. Senart<sup>6</sup> has emphasized this fact as a basis of caste,
<div n="lb"/>on the ground that marriage within a {%curia, phratria,%} or caste
<div n="lb"/>({@Varṇa@}) was Indo-European, as was marriage outside the circle
<div n="lb"/>of agnates and cognates. But there is no evidence at all<sup>7</sup> to
<div n="lb"/>prove that this practice was Indo-European, while in India the
<div n="lb"/>Śatapatha<sup>8</sup> expressly recognizes marriage within the third or
<div n="lb"/>fourth degree on either side. According to Sāyaṇa, the Kāṇvas
<div n="lb"/>accepted marriage in the third degree, the Saurāṣṭras only in
<div n="lb"/>the fourth, while the scholiast on the Vajrasūcī<sup>9</sup> adḍs to the
<div n="lb"/>Kāṇvas the Andhras and the Dākṣiṇātyas, and remarks that
<div n="lb"/>the Vājasaneyins forbade marriage with the daughter of the
<div n="lb"/>mother's brother. All apparently allowed marriage with the
<div n="lb"/>daughter of a paternal uncle, which later was quite excluded.
<div n="lb"/>Change of Gotra was quite possible, as in the case of {@Śunaḥ-@}
<div n="lb"/>{@śepa@} and {@Gṛtsamada,@} who, once an Aṅgirasa, became a
<div n="lb"/>Bhārgava.<sup>10</sup>
