{@Dhiṣaṇā,@}¦ according to the St. Petersburg Dictionary, denotes<sup>1</sup>
<div n="lb"/>an implement used in preparing the Soma, ‘bowl’ or ‘vat,’
<div n="lb"/>and by metonymy also the Soma draught itself.<sup>2</sup> The dual, by
[Page1-400+ 54]
<div n="lb"/>a metaphor,<sup>3</sup> also expresses the ‘two worlds,’ heaven and earth.<sup>4</sup>
<div n="lb"/>Hillebrandt,<sup>5</sup> however, thinks that the word properly means
<div n="lb"/>earth,<sup>6</sup> in the dual heaven and earth,<sup>7</sup> in the plural the triad,
<div n="lb"/>earth, atmosphere, and heaven,<sup>8</sup> while in some passages<sup>9</sup>
<div n="lb"/>Dhiṣaṇā denotes the Vedi, the excavated ground used as an
<div n="lb"/>altar. This is not, however, certain, while it seems clear that
<div n="lb"/>the Vājasaneyi<sup>10</sup> and Taittirīya<sup>11</sup> Saṃhitās understand the
<div n="lb"/>Dhiṣaṇās (dual) to be the planks over which the pressing of
<div n="lb"/>the Soma took place ({%adhiṣavaṇa-phalake%}).<sup>12</sup> Pischel<sup>13</sup> sees in
<div n="lb"/>Dhiṣaṇā a goddess of wealth akin to Aditi and the earth.
