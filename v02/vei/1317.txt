{@Diś,@}¦ ‘direction,’ is a word very frequently used in the Rigveda
<div n="lb"/>and later<sup>1</sup> to denote a quarter of the sky. As a general rule,
<div n="lb"/>four quarters are mentioned — east, south, west, north.<sup>2</sup> But
<div n="lb"/>the number of the ‘directions’ is sometimes increased up to
<div n="lb"/>ten by the addition to these four of various others. The five
<div n="lb"/>points include the zenith ({%ūrdhvā%});<sup>3</sup> the six, the zenith and
<div n="lb"/>the nadir ({%ūrdhvā%} and {%avācī%});<sup>4</sup> the seven, the zenith, the
<div n="lb"/>ground on which one stands ({%dhruvā%}), and the air ({%antarikṣa%})
<div n="lb"/>between these two ({%vyadhvā%});<sup>5</sup> the eight include the inter-
<div n="lb"/>mediate quarters (S.E., S.W., N.E., N.W.);<sup>6</sup> the nine add to
<div n="lb"/>these the zenith;<sup>7</sup> the ten, zenith and nadir.<sup>8</sup> The number
[Page1-366+ 56]
<div n="lb"/>five is sometimes made up by the ground beneath the observer's
<div n="lb"/>feet ({%dhruvā%}),<sup>9</sup> and the number six by that point ({%dhruvā%}) and
<div n="lb"/>the zenith ({%ūrdhvā%});<sup>10</sup> the ‘lofty’ ({%bṛhatī%})<sup>11</sup> sometimes taking
<div n="lb"/>the place of the ‘vertical’ ({%ūrdhvā%}).
