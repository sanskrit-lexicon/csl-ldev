{@Mṛtyu,@}¦ ‘death,’ is repeatedly mentioned in the Rigveda<sup>1</sup>
<div n="lb"/>and later<sup>2</sup> as a thing of terror. There are a hundred and one
<div n="lb"/>forms of death, the natural one by old age<sup>3</sup> ({%jarā%}), and a
<div n="lb"/>hundred others, all to be avoided.<sup>4</sup> To die before old age
<div n="lb"/>({%purā jarasaḥ%})<sup>5</sup> is to die before the allotted span ({%purā āyuṣaḥ%}),<sup>6</sup>
<div n="lb"/>the normal length of life being throughout Vedic literature
<div n="lb"/>spoken of as a hundred years.<sup>7</sup> On the other hand, the evils
<div n="lb"/>of old age in the loss of physical strength were clearly realized.<sup>8</sup>
<div n="lb"/>one of the feats of the Aśvins was to restore old {@Cyavāna@} to
<div n="lb"/>his former youth and powers, and another was the rejuvenation
<div n="lb"/>of {@Kali.@}<sup>9</sup> The Atharvaveda<sup>10</sup> is full of charms of all sorts to
<div n="lb"/>avert death and secure length of years ({%āyuṣya%}).
<div n="P"/>The modes of disposing of the dead were burial and cremation
<div n="lb"/>(see {@Agnidagdha@}). Both existed in the early Vedic period,<sup>11</sup>
<div n="lb"/>as in Greece;<sup>12</sup> but the former method was on the whole less
<div n="lb"/>favoured, and tended to be regarded with disapproval. The
<div n="lb"/>bones of the dead, whether burned or not, were marked by the
<div n="lb"/>erection of a tumulus ({@Śmaśāna@}): the Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa<sup>13</sup>
<div n="lb"/>preserves traces of strong differences of opinion as to the mode
<div n="lb"/>in which these tumuli should be constructed. There is little
<div n="lb"/>or no trace<sup>14</sup> of the custom common in northern lands of
<div n="lb"/>sending the dead man to sea in a burning ship: the reference
[Page2-176+ 60]
<div n="lb"/>to a ship<sup>15</sup> seems to point to mythical perils after death, not to
<div n="lb"/>the mode of burial.
<div n="P"/>The life after death was to the Vedic Indian a repetition of
<div n="lb"/>the life in this world. He passed into the next world {%sarva-%}
<div n="lb"/>{%tanuḥ sāṅgaḥ,%} ‘with whole body and all his members,’<sup>16</sup> enjoying
<div n="lb"/>there the same pleasures as he had enjoyed on earth. Even
<div n="lb"/>in the Rigveda<sup>17</sup> there are hints of evil awaiting evil-doers, but
<div n="lb"/>it is not until the Atharvaveda<sup>18</sup> and the Brāhmaṇas<sup>19</sup> that
<div n="lb"/>a hell of punishment is set out, and it is in the Brāhmaṇas<sup>20</sup>
<div n="lb"/>that good and evil deeds are said to produce happiness or hell
<div n="lb"/>hereafter. But there is no hint of extinction<sup>21</sup> in the Rigveda
<div n="lb"/>as the fate of the wicked, as Roth<sup>22</sup> inclined to think. The
<div n="lb"/>Vedic poet not being deeply moral, his verses do not convey,
<div n="lb"/>as would those of a man convinced of sin, warnings of future
<div n="lb"/>judgment.
