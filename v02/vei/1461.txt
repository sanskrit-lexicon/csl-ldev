{@Dhmātṛ@}¦ (lit. ‘blower’) occurs twice in one passage of the
Rigveda<sup>1</sup> in the two forms, {%dhmātā,%} nom. ‘smelter,’ and
{%dhmātarī,%} which, according to the Padapāṭha, stands for
{%dhmātari,%} a locative probably meaning ‘in the smelting furnace.’<sup>2</sup>
Geldner,<sup>3</sup> Bartholomae,<sup>4</sup> and Oldenburg<sup>5</sup> regard the latter form
as a locative infinitive, ‘in the smelting.’ Ludwig<sup>6</sup> and Neisser<sup>7</sup>
think {%dhmātarī%} is a nom. sing. masc. used in the same sense as
{%dhmātā.%} Smelting is also clearly referred to,<sup>8</sup> and the smelter
is described as using the wings of birds ({%parṇa śakunānām%}) to
fan the flame.<sup>9</sup> That the art was widely applied is shown by
the fact that reference is made to arrows with points of {@Ayas,@}<sup>10</sup>
to kettles which were fashioned of the same metal and could be
placed upon a fire,<sup>11</sup> and to Soma cups of beaten Ayas.<sup>12</sup>
