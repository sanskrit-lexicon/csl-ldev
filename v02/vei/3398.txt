{@Śvaśura@}¦ from the Rigveda onwards<sup>1</sup> denotes the ‘father-in-
<div n="lb"/>law’ of the wife; not till the Sūtra period does it include the
<div n="lb"/>‘father-in-law’ of the husband.<sup>2</sup> The daughter-in-law ({@Snuṣā@}),
<div n="lb"/>in the normal case when the father-in-law was the head of the
<div n="lb"/>family to which her husband belonged in fact as well as in age,
<div n="lb"/>was bound to pay him all respect.<sup>3</sup> When the old man had
<div n="lb"/>ceased to exercise control, she became mistress ({%samrājñī%})
<div n="lb"/>over him and his wife.<sup>4</sup> In the plural<sup>5</sup> the word denotes the
<div n="lb"/>‘parents-in-law.’
