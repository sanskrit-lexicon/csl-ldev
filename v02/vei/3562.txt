<L>3562<pc>2-447<k1>सालावृक<k2>सालावृक
{@Sālā-vṛka@}¦ is found twice in the Rigveda<sup>1</sup> apparently denoting
the ‘hyæna’ or ‘wild dog.’ This sense also seems appropriate
in the later narrative of the destruction of the {@Yatis@} by Indra,<sup>2</sup>
who is said to have handed them over to the Sālāvṛkas. Sālā-
vṛkeya<sup>3</sup> is a variant form of the same word, meaning literally
‘descendant of a Sālāvṛka.’ The feminine is Sālāvṛkī,<sup>4</sup> but in
the Taittirīya Saṃhitā<sup>5</sup> it appears as Salāvṛkī. {%Cf.%} {@Tarakṣu.@}
<F>1) x. 73, 2; 95, 15.</F>
<F>2) Taittirīya Saṃhitā, vi. 2, 7, 5;
Aitareya Brāhmaṇa, vii. 28, 1; Kauṣī-
taki Upaniṣad, iii. 1 ({%varia lectio%}).</F>
<F>3) Pañcaviṃśa Brāhmaṇa, viii. 1, 4;
xiii. 4, 16; xiv. 11, 28; xviii. 1, 9;
xix. 4, 7; Jaiminīya Brāhmaṇa, i. 185
