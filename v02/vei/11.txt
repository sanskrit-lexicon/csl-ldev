<L>11<pc>1-006<k1>अगस्त्य<k2>अगस्त्य
{@Agastya.@}¦ — This is the name of a sage, of mythical character,
<div n="lb"/>who plays a great part in the later literature. He was a {@Māna,@}<sup>1</sup>
<div n="lb"/>and therefore is called {@Mānya@}<sup>2</sup> and son of Māna, and only once
<div n="lb"/>is there a reference<sup>3</sup> to the legend prevalent in latter times that
<div n="lb"/>he was a son of Mitra and Varuṇa.
<div n="P"/>His greatest feat was the reconciliation of Indra and the
<div n="lb"/>Maruts after Indra had been annoyed at his proposing to give
<div n="lb"/>the Maruts an offering to the exclusion of Indra. This feat is
<div n="lb"/>the subject of three hymns of the Rigveda,<sup>4</sup> and is often referred
[Page1-007+ 62]
<div n="lb"/>to in the Brāhmaṇas,<sup>5</sup> though the exact details and significance
<div n="lb"/>of the legend are variously treated by Oldenberg,<sup>6</sup> Sieg,<sup>7</sup> Hertel,<sup>8</sup>
<div n="lb"/>and von Schroeder.<sup>9</sup>
<div n="P"/>He also appears in a strange dialogue with Lopāmudrā in
<div n="lb"/>the Rigveda,<sup>10</sup> which appears to show him as an ascetic who
<div n="lb"/>finally yields to temptation. Von {@Schroeder@}<sup>11</sup> regards it as
<div n="lb"/>a ritual drama of vegetation magic.
<div n="P"/>In another passage of the Rigveda<sup>12</sup> he appears as helping
<div n="lb"/>in the Aśvins’ gift of a leg to Viśpalā. Sāyaṇa holds that be
<div n="lb"/>was the Purohita of {@Khela,@} and Sieg<sup>13</sup> accepts this view, while
<div n="lb"/>Pischel<sup>14</sup> thinks that Khela is a deity, Vivasvant.
<div n="P"/>Geldner<sup>15</sup> shows from the Rigveda<sup>16</sup> that Agastya, as brother
<div n="lb"/>of Vasiṣṭha — both being miraculous sons of Mitra and Varuṇa
<div n="lb"/>-introduces {@Vasiṣṭha@} to the {@Tṛtsus.@} There are two other
<div n="lb"/>references to Agastya in the Rigveda, the one<sup>17</sup> including him
<div n="lb"/>in a long list of persons, the other alluding to his sister's sons
<div n="lb"/>({%nadbhyaḥ%}),<sup>18</sup> apparently {@Bandhu,@} etc. In the Atharvaveda<sup>19</sup>
<div n="lb"/>he appears as connected with witchcraft, and in a long list of
<div n="lb"/>sages.<sup>20</sup> In the Maitrāyaṇī Saṃhitā<sup>21</sup> cows, with a peculiar
<div n="lb"/>mark on their ears ({%viṣṭya-karṇyaḥ%}), are associated with him.
