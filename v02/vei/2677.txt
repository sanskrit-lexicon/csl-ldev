<L>2677<pc>2-219<k1>राजसूय<k2>राजसूय
{@Rāja-sūya@}¦ is the name in the Atharvaveda<sup>1</sup> and the later
<div n="lb"/>literature<sup>2</sup> of the ceremony of the ‘royal consecration.’ The
<div n="lb"/>rite is described at great length in the Sūtras,<sup>3</sup> but its main
<div n="lb"/>features are clearly outlined in the Brāhmaṇas,<sup>4</sup> while the
<div n="lb"/>verses used in the ceremony are preserved in the Saṃhitās of
<div n="lb"/>the Yajurveda.<sup>5</sup> Besides much mere priestly elaboration, the
<div n="lb"/>ritual contains traces of popular ceremonial. For example, the
<div n="lb"/>king is clothed in the ceremonial garments of his rank, and
<div n="lb"/>provided with bow and arrow as emblems of sovereignty. He
<div n="lb"/>is formally anointed; he performs a mimic cow raid against a
<div n="lb"/>relative of his;<sup>6</sup> or engages in a sham fight with a {@Rājanya.@}<sup>7</sup>
<div n="lb"/>A game of dice is played in which he is made to be the victim;<sup>8</sup>
<div n="lb"/>he symbolically ascends the quarters of the sky as an indication
<div n="lb"/>of his universal rule; and steps on a tiger skin, thus gaining
<div n="lb"/>the strength and the pre-eminence of the tiger.
