{@Vanas-pati,@}¦ ‘lord of the forest,’ primarily denotes ‘tree,’<sup>1</sup> and
then ‘post’ or ‘pole.’<sup>2</sup> In some passages it is applied either
to a part of the chariot or to the chariot as a whole.<sup>3</sup> It also
means a ‘wooden drum’<sup>4</sup> and a ‘wooden amulet,’<sup>5</sup> while in
some passages<sup>6</sup> it denotes the plant {%par excellence,%} Soma.
<F>1) Rv. i. 166, 5; iii. 34, 10; v. 7,
4; 41, 8, etc.; Av. xi. 6, 1 (distin-
guished from {@Vīrudh@} and {@Oradhi@});
9, 24, etc.</F>
<F>2) Taittirīya Saṃhitā, vi. 2, 8, 4;
Av. ix. 3, 11, etc.</F>
<F>3) Rv. ii. 37, 3; iii. 53, 20; vi. 47,
26; Nirukta, ix. 11. See Zimmer,
{%Altindisches Leben,%} 251.</F>
<F>4) Vājasaneyi Saṃhitā, ix. 12. {%Cf.%}
Av. xii. 3, 15.</F>
<F>5) Av. vi. 85, 1; x. 3, 8. 11.</F>
<F>6) Rv. i. 91, 6; Vājasaneyi Saṃhitā,
x. 23, etc.</F>
