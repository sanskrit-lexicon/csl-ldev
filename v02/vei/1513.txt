<L>1513<pc>1-435<k1>नपात्<k2>नपात्
{@Napāt@}¦ in Vedic literature apparently has both the wider
sense of ‘descendant,’<sup>1</sup> and the narrower one of ‘grandson’ in
the Saṃhitās.<sup>2</sup> In the Brāhmaṇas the word seems hardly to
have the sense of ‘descendant’ at all, while it denotes not
only ‘grandson,’<sup>3</sup> but also ‘great-grandson’ in the sequence
‘sons, grandsons, great-grandsons’ ({%putrān, pautrān, naptṝn%}).<sup>4</sup>
‘Grandson’ is also expressed by Pautra (‘son's son’) in the
Atharvaveda and later,<sup>5</sup> while the sense of ‘great-grandson’ is
accurately conveyed as early as the Rigveda<sup>6</sup> by Pra-ṇapāt,
used beside Napāt, ‘grandson.’ Naptī, the feminine, is practi-
cally limited to the Saṃhitās,<sup>7</sup> and denotes ‘daughter.’ The
use in the Veda throws no light on the original use of the
word.<sup>8</sup>
