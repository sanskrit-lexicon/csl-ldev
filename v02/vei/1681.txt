{@Pari-kṣit@}¦ appears in the Atharvaveda<sup>1</sup> as a king in whose
<div n="lb"/>realm, that of the Kurus, prosperity and peace abound. The
<div n="lb"/>verses in which he is celebrated are later<sup>2</sup> called {%Pārikṣityaḥ,%}
<div n="lb"/>and the Brāhmaṇas explain that Agni is {%pari-kṣit%} because he
<div n="lb"/>dwells among men. Hence Roth<sup>3</sup> and Bloomfield<sup>4</sup> regard
<div n="lb"/>Parikṣit in the Atharvaveda not as a human king at all. This
<div n="lb"/>may be correct, but it is not certain. Both Zimmer<sup>5</sup> and
[Page1-494+ 45]
<div n="lb"/>Oldenberg<sup>6</sup> recognize Parikṣit as a real king, a view supported
<div n="lb"/>by the fact that in the later Vedic literature King {@Janamejaya@}
<div n="lb"/>bears the patronymic {@Pārikṣita.@} If this be so, Parikṣit
<div n="lb"/>belonged to the later period, since the Atharvan passage in
<div n="lb"/>which his name occurs is certainly late, and none of the other
<div n="lb"/>Saṃhitās know Parikṣit at all. The Epic<sup>7</sup> makes him grandfather
<div n="lb"/>of Pratiśravas and great-grandfather of Pratīpa, and Zimmer,<sup>5</sup>
<div n="lb"/>probably with justice, compares the Prātisutvana and Pratīpa
<div n="lb"/>found in another late Atharvan passage.<sup>8</sup> But {@Devāpi@} and
<div n="lb"/>{@Śantanu@} cannot be brought into connexion with Pratīpa.<sup>9</sup>
