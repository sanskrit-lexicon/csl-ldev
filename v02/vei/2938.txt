{@Vidatha@}¦ is a word of obscure sense, confined mainly to the
<div n="lb"/>Rigveda. According to Roth,<sup>1</sup> the sense is primarily ‘order,’
<div n="lb"/>then the concrete body which gives orders, then ‘assembly’ for
<div n="lb"/>secular<sup>2</sup> or religious ends,<sup>3</sup> or for war.<sup>4</sup> Oldenberg<sup>5</sup> once
<div n="lb"/>thought that the main idea is ‘ordinance’ (from {%vi-dhā,%}
<div n="lb"/>‘dispose,’ ‘ordain’), and thence ‘sacrifice.’ Ludwig<sup>6</sup> thinks
<div n="lb"/>that the root idea is an ‘assembly,’ especially of the {@Maghavans@}
<div n="lb"/>and the Brahmins. Geldner<sup>7</sup> considers that the word pri-
<div n="lb"/>marily means ‘knowledge,’ ‘wisdom,’ ‘priestly lore,’ then
<div n="lb"/>‘sacrifice’ and ‘spiritual authority.’ Bloomfield,<sup>8</sup> on the other
<div n="lb"/>hand, insists that Vidatha refers to the ‘house’<sup>9</sup> in the first
<div n="lb"/>place (from {%vid,%} ‘acquire’), and then to the ‘sacrifice,’ as
<div n="lb"/>connected with the house; this interpretation, at any rate,
<div n="lb"/>appears to suit all the passages. The term {%vidathya,%} once<sup>10</sup>
<div n="lb"/>applied to the king ({%samrāṭ%}), might seem to be against this
<div n="lb"/>view, but it may refer to his being ‘rich in homesteads’; and
<div n="lb"/>the connexion of the woman with the Vidatha, as opposed to
[Page2-297+ 43]
<div n="lb"/>the {@Sabhā,@} tells in favour<sup>11</sup> of Bloomfield's explanation. That
<div n="lb"/>the word ever denotes an asylum, like the house of the
<div n="lb"/>Brahmin,<sup>12</sup> as Ludwig<sup>13</sup> suggests, is doubtful.<sup>14</sup>
