<L>171<pc>1-042<k1>अश्व<k2>अश्व
{@Aśva@}¦ is the commonest word for ‘horse’ in the Vedic
literature. The horse is also called ‘the runner’ ({%atya%}), ‘the
swift’ ({%arvant%}), ‘the strong,’ for pulling ({%vājin%}), ‘the runner’
({%sapti%}), and ‘the speeding’ ({%haya%}). The mare is termed {%aśvā,%}
{%atyā, arvatī, vaḍavā,%} etc. Horses of various colour were known,
dun ({%harita, hari%}), ruddy ({%aruṇa, aruṣa, piśaṅga, rohita%}), dark
brown ({%śyāva%}), white ({%śveta%}), etc. A white horse with black
ears is mentioned in the Atharvaveda as of special value.<sup>1</sup>
Horses were highly prized,<sup>2</sup> and were not rare, as Roth<sup>3</sup>
thought, for as many as four hundred mares are mentioned in
one Dānastuti (‘Praise of Gifts’).<sup>4</sup> They were on occasion
ornamented with pearls and gold.<sup>5</sup>
<div n="P"/>Mares were preferred for drawing chariots because of their
swiftness and sureness.<sup>6</sup> They were also used for drawing
carts, but were not ordinarily so employed.<sup>7</sup> No mention is
made of riding in battle, but for other purposes it was not
unknown.<sup>8</sup>
<div n="P"/>Horses were often kept in stalls,<sup>9</sup> and fed there.<sup>10</sup> But they
were also allowed to go out to grass,<sup>11</sup> and were then hobbled.<sup>12</sup>
They were watered to cool them after racing.<sup>13</sup> Their attendants
[Page1-043+ 59]
are frequently referred to ({%aśva-pāla,%}<sup>14</sup> {%aśva-pa,%}<sup>15</sup> {%aśva-pati%}).<sup>16</sup>
Stallions were frequently castrated ({%vadhri%}).<sup>17</sup>
<div n="P"/>Besides reins ({%raśmayaḥ%}), reference is made to halters
({%aśvābhidhānī%}),<sup>18</sup> and whips ({%aśvājani%}).<sup>19</sup> See also {@Ratha.@}
<div n="P"/>Horses from the Indus were of special value,<sup>20</sup> as also horses
from the {@Sarasvatī.@}
