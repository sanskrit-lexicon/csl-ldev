{@Bhiṣaj,@}¦ ‘physician,’ is a word of common occurrence in the
<div n="lb"/>Rigveda<sup>1</sup> and later.<sup>2</sup> There is no trace whatever in the former
<div n="lb"/>text of the profession being held in disrepute: the Aśvins,<sup>3</sup>
<div n="lb"/>Varuṇa,<sup>4</sup> and Rudra<sup>5</sup> are all called physicians. On the other
<div n="lb"/>hand, in the Dharma literature<sup>6</sup> this profession is utterly
[Page2-105+ 54]
<div n="lb"/>despised. This dislike is found as early as the Yajurveda
<div n="lb"/>Saṃhitās,<sup>7</sup> where the Aśvins are condemned because of their
<div n="lb"/>having to do with the practice of medicine ({%bheṣaja%}), on the
<div n="lb"/>ground that it brings them too much among men, an allusion
<div n="lb"/>to the caste dislike of promiscuous contact.
<div n="P"/>The Rigveda<sup>8</sup> contains a hymn in which a physician cele-
<div n="lb"/>brates his plants and their healing powers. Moreover, wonder-
<div n="lb"/>ful cures are referred to as performed by the Aśvins: the
<div n="lb"/>healing of the lame<sup>9</sup> and of the blind;<sup>10</sup> the rejuvenation of the
<div n="lb"/>aged {@Cyavana@}<sup>11</sup> and of {@Puraṃdhi's@} husband;<sup>12</sup> the giving of an
<div n="lb"/>iron leg ({%jaṅghā āyasī%}) to {@Viśpalā,@}<sup>13</sup> a deed only more wonderful
<div n="lb"/>if we assume that Viśpalā was a mare, as has been suggested by
<div n="lb"/>Pischel.<sup>14</sup> It would in all probability be a mistake to assume<sup>15</sup>
<div n="lb"/>that the Vedic Indians had any surgical skill: they no doubt
<div n="lb"/>applied simples to wounds,<sup>16</sup> but both their medicine and their
<div n="lb"/>surgery must have been most primitive. All that the Atharva-
<div n="lb"/>veda shows in regard to medicine is the use of herbs combined
<div n="lb"/>with spells,<sup>17</sup> and of water ({%cf.%} {@Jalāṣa@}), remedies Indo-European
<div n="lb"/>in character, but not of much scientific value. On the other
<div n="lb"/>hand, the knowledge of anatomy shown (see {@Śarīra@}), though
<div n="lb"/>betraying grave inaccuracies, is not altogether insignificant;
<div n="lb"/>but that was due no doubt mainly to the practice of dissecting
<div n="lb"/>animals at the sacrifice.
<div n="P"/>There is some evidence in the Rigveda<sup>18</sup> that the practice of
<div n="lb"/>medicine was already a profession; this is supported by the
<div n="lb"/>inclusion of a physician in the list of victims at the Puruṣa-
<div n="lb"/>medha (‘human sacrifice’) in the Yajurveda.<sup>19</sup> According to
[Page2-106+ 48]
<div n="lb"/>Bloomfield,<sup>20</sup> a hymn of the Atharvaveda<sup>21</sup> contains a physician's
<div n="lb"/>deprecation of the use of home-made remedies instead of
<div n="lb"/>reliance on his professional training.
