<L>325<pc>1-081<k1>इषु<k2>इषु
{@Iṣu@}¦ is the usual name for ‘arrow’ from the Rigveda<sup>1</sup>
<div n="lb"/>onwards. Other names are {@Śarya, Śārī,@} and {@Bāṇa.@} In the
<div n="lb"/>hymn of the Rigveda,<sup>2</sup> which gives a catalogue of armour,
<div n="lb"/>two kinds of arrows are distinctly referred to: the one is
<div n="lb"/>poisoned ({%ālāktā%}), and has a head of horn ({%ruru-śīrṣṇī%}); the
<div n="lb"/>other is copper-, bronze-, or iron-headed ({%ayo-mukham%}). Poisoned
<div n="lb"/>({%digdhā%}) arrows are also referred to in the Atharvaveda.<sup>3</sup> The
<div n="lb"/>arrows were feathered.<sup>4</sup> The parts of an arrow are enumerated
<div n="lb"/>in the Atharvaveda<sup>5</sup> as the shaft ({%śalya%}), the feather-socket
<div n="lb"/>({%parṇa-dhi%}), the point ({%śṛṅga%}), the neck of the point in which
<div n="lb"/>the shaft is fixed ({%kulmala%}), and the {@Apaskambha@} and {@Apāṣṭha,@}
<div n="lb"/>which are of more doubtful significance. In the Aitareya
<div n="lb"/>Brāhmaṇa<sup>6</sup> the parts of an arrow are given as the point ({%anīka%}),
<div n="lb"/>the {%śalya, tejana,%} and the feathers ({%parṇāni%}), where {%śalya%} and
<div n="lb"/>{%tejana%} must apparently mean the upper and lower parts of the
<div n="lb"/>shaft, since it is reasonable to suppose that the arrow is
<div n="lb"/>described as a whole consecutively. So in the Atharvaveda<sup>7</sup>
<div n="lb"/>the arrow of Kāma is described as having feathers, a shaft
[Page1-082+ 56]
<div n="lb"/>({%śalya%}), and a firm fastening ({%kulmala%}).<sup>8</sup> The arrow was shot
<div n="lb"/>from the ear, and so is described in the Rigveda<sup>9</sup> as ‘having
<div n="lb"/>the ear for its place of birth.’
<div n="P"/>As a measure of length, the Iṣu was five spans, say three feet.<sup>10</sup>
