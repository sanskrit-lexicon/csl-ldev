<L>3643<pc>2-462<k1>सूत<k2>सूत
{@Sūta@}¦ is the name of a court official who is often mentioned
<div n="lb"/>with the {@Grāmaṇī.@} He is one of the eight {@Vīras@} in the Pañca-
<div n="lb"/>viṃśa Brāhmaṇa,<sup>1</sup> and of the eleven {@Ratnins@} in other texts.<sup>2</sup>
<div n="lb"/>He also appears in the Atharvaveda<sup>3</sup> among the kingmakers
<div n="lb"/>({@Rājakṛt@}) and in the Śatrarudriya<sup>4</sup> (‘section dealing with the
<div n="lb"/>hundred Rudras’) of the Yajurveda. The commentators are
<div n="lb"/>agreed in seeing in him the ‘charioteer’ ({@Sārathi@}) or ‘master of
<div n="lb"/>the horse,’ of the king; this sense is accepted by Roth,<sup>5</sup> by
<div n="lb"/>Whitney,<sup>6</sup> and by Bloomfield.<sup>7</sup> But the fact that the {@Saṃ-@}
[Page2-463+ 49]
<div n="lb"/>{@grahītṛ,@} who occurs in several passages beside the Sūta, is the
<div n="lb"/>‘charioteer,’ renders this version improbable. Eggeling<sup>8</sup> thinks
<div n="lb"/>that he was, in the Brāhmaṇas at least, a minstrel and court
<div n="lb"/>poet, while Weber<sup>9</sup> considers that his name denotes him as
<div n="lb"/>‘consecrated’ — that is, one who has constant access to the
<div n="lb"/>king. In the Epic the Sūta serves as a royal herald and
<div n="lb"/>bard:<sup>10</sup> it may be that the curious words {%ahanti,%}<sup>11</sup> {%ahantya,%}<sup>12</sup> or
<div n="lb"/>{%ahantva%}<sup>13</sup> applied to him<sup>14</sup> in the Śatarudriya denote his sacred
<div n="lb"/>character at once as minstrel and as herald — a combination of
<div n="lb"/>functions not unknown elsewhere.
