{@Paḍ-bīśa,@}¦ the ‘foot-fetter’ of a horse in five passages, two
<div n="lb"/>in the Rigveda,<sup>1</sup> and one each in the Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad,<sup>2</sup>
<div n="lb"/>the Chāndogya Upaniṣad,<sup>3</sup> and the Śāṅkhāyana Āraṇyaka.<sup>4</sup>
<div n="lb"/>Elsewhere<sup>5</sup> its uses are metaphorical. According to Roth,<sup>6</sup> the
<div n="lb"/>literal sense is ‘foot-fastening’ ({%paḍ%} being = {%pad,%} ‘foot,’ and
<div n="lb"/>{%bīśa,%} written {%vīśa%} in the Vājasaneyi Saṃhitā, being connected
<div n="lb"/>with the Latin {%vincire,%} ‘bind’). Pischel<sup>7</sup> objects that the
<div n="lb"/>sense of ‘foot-fastening’ involves the absurdity, in the Upaniṣad
<div n="lb"/>passages, of a fine horse from the {@Sindhu@} (Indus) being spoken
<div n="lb"/>of as tearing up the peg to which it is fastened. He suggests
<div n="lb"/>instead the meaning of ‘hobble,’ which must be right.<sup>8</sup>
