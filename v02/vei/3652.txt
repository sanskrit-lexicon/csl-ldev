{@Sūrya,@}¦ the ‘sun,’ plays a great part in Vedic mythology and
<div n="lb"/>religion,<sup>1</sup> corresponding with the importance of the sun as a
<div n="lb"/>factor in the physical life of the peninsula. In the Rigveda<sup>2</sup>
<div n="lb"/>the sun is normally regarded as a beneficent power, a not
<div n="lb"/>unnatural view in a people which must apparently have issued
<div n="lb"/>from the cold regions of the Himālaya mountains. Its heat
<div n="lb"/>is, however, alluded to in some passages of the Rigveda,<sup>3</sup> as
<div n="lb"/>well as referred to in the Atharvaveda and the literature of the
<div n="lb"/>Brāhmaṇas.<sup>4</sup>
<div n="P"/>In one myth Indra is said to have vanquished Sūrya and to
<div n="lb"/>have stolen his wheel:<sup>5</sup> this is possibly a reference to the
[Page2-466+ 62]
<div n="lb"/>obscuration of the sun by a thunderstorm.<sup>6</sup> The Aitareya
<div n="lb"/>Brāhmaṇa<sup>7</sup> presents a naive conception of the course of the
<div n="lb"/>sun, which it regards as bright on one side only, and as
<div n="lb"/>returning from west to east by the same road, but with the
<div n="lb"/>reverse side turned towards the earth, thus at night illumining
<div n="lb"/>the stars in heaven.<sup>8</sup> In the Rigveda<sup>9</sup> wonder is expressed
<div n="lb"/>that the sun does not fall.
<div n="P"/>There are several references to eclipses in the Rigveda. In
<div n="lb"/>one passage<sup>10</sup> Svarbhānu, a demon, is said to have eclipsed the
<div n="lb"/>sun with darkness, while {@Atri@} restores the light of the sun, a
<div n="lb"/>similar feat being elsewhere attributed to his family, the Atris.<sup>11</sup>
<div n="lb"/>In the Atharvaveda<sup>12</sup> {@Rāhu@} appears for the first time in con-
<div n="lb"/>nexion with the sun. Indra's defeat of Sūrya<sup>6</sup> may also be
<div n="lb"/>explained as alluding to an eclipse; in two other passages<sup>13</sup>
<div n="lb"/>such an interpretation seems at least probable. Ludwig<sup>14</sup> not
<div n="lb"/>only argues that the Rigveda knows the theory of eclipses
<div n="lb"/>caused by an occultation of the sun by the moon, and regards
<div n="lb"/>the sun as going round the earth,<sup>15</sup> but even endeavours to
<div n="lb"/>identify an eclipse referred to in the Rigveda with one that
<div n="lb"/>occurred in 1029 B.C. These views are completely refuted by
<div n="lb"/>Whitney.<sup>16</sup>
<div n="P"/>The sun as a maker of time<sup>17</sup> determines the year of 360 days,
<div n="lb"/>which is the civil year and the usual year ({@Saṃvatsara@}) of
[Page2-467+ 70]
<div n="lb"/>Vedic literature. This solar year is divided into two halves — 
<div n="lb"/>the Uttarāyaṇa,<sup>18</sup> when the sun goes north, and the Dakṣiṇā-
<div n="lb"/>yana,<sup>19</sup> when it goes south. There can be no doubt that these
<div n="lb"/>periods denote the time when the sun turns north from the
<div n="lb"/>winter solstice, and when it turns south from the summer
<div n="lb"/>solstice, for the Kauṣītaki Brāhmaṇa<sup>20</sup> says so in perfectly clear
<div n="lb"/>language. The alternative theory is to regard the periods as
<div n="lb"/>those when the sun is in the north — {%i.e.,%} when it is north of the
<div n="lb"/>equator, and when it is in the south, taking as points of depar-
<div n="lb"/>ture the equinoxes, not the solstices; but this view has no
<div n="lb"/>support in Vedic literature, and is opposed to the fact that
<div n="lb"/>the equinoxes play no part in Vedic astronomical theory.<sup>21</sup>
<div n="lb"/>There are only doubtful references to the solstices in the
<div n="lb"/>Rigveda.<sup>22</sup>
<div n="P"/>The Brāhmaṇas,<sup>23</sup> and perhaps the Rigveda,<sup>24</sup> regard the
<div n="lb"/>moon as entering the sun at new moon. According to Hille-
<div n="lb"/>brandt,<sup>25</sup> the Rigveda<sup>26</sup> recognizes that the moon shines
<div n="lb"/>by the borrowed light of the sun, but this seems very doubt-
[Page2-468+ 54]
<div n="lb"/>ful. See also {@Aryamṇaḥ Panthā,@}<sup>27</sup> {@Nakṣatra,@} and {@Sapta@}
