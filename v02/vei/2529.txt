{@Medhātithi,@}¦<sup>1</sup> {@Medhyātithi@}<sup>2</sup> (‘having a guest at the sacrifice’)
<div n="lb"/>appear to be the names of one and the same man, a descendant
<div n="lb"/>of {@Kaṇva@} and a famous Vedic {@Ṛṣi,@} to whom the authorship of
<div n="lb"/>various hymns<sup>3</sup> is attributed in the Anukramaṇī (Index). To
<div n="lb"/>him Indra is said in the Rigveda<sup>4</sup> to have come in the form
<div n="lb"/>of a ram: this myth is perpetuated in the Subrahmaṇyā
<div n="lb"/>formula<sup>5</sup> recited by the priest while the Soma is being carried
<div n="lb"/>within the sacrificial enclosure, when Indra is hailed as ‘ram
<div n="lb"/>of Medhātithi.’ He appears also as a rival of {@Vatsa,@} whom he
<div n="lb"/>accused of low birth, but who convinced him of his error by
<div n="lb"/>undergoing a fire ordeal ({%cf.%} {@Divya@}).<sup>6</sup> In the Atharvaveda<sup>7</sup> he
<div n="lb"/>is mentioned with many other sages, and occurs elsewhere<sup>8</sup>
<div n="lb"/>also as a sage.
