{@Pārikṣita,@}¦ ‘descendant of {@Parikṣit,@}’ is the patronymic of
<div n="lb"/>{@Janamejaya@} in the Aitareya Brāhmaṇa<sup>1</sup> and the Śatapatha
<div n="lb"/>Brāhmaṇa.<sup>2</sup> The Pārikṣitīya appear in the Śatapatha Brāh-
<div n="lb"/>maṇa<sup>3</sup> and the Śāṅkhāyana Śrauta Sūtra<sup>4</sup> as performers of the
<div n="lb"/>horse sacrifice. In a Gāthā there cited they are called Pāri-
<div n="lb"/>kṣitas. Apparently they were the brothers of Janamejaya, named
<div n="lb"/>{@Ugrasena, Bhīmasena,@} and {@Śrutasena.@} In the Bṛhadāraṇyaka
<div n="lb"/>Upaniṣad<sup>5</sup> the question whither they have gone is made the
<div n="lb"/>subject of a philosophical discussion. It is clear that the family
<div n="lb"/>had passed away before the time of the Upaniṣad, and it is also
<div n="lb"/>clear that there had been some serious scandal mingled with
<div n="lb"/>their greatness which they had, in the opinion of the Brahmins,
<div n="lb"/>atoned for by their horse sacrifice with its boundless gifts to
<div n="lb"/>the priests. Weber<sup>6</sup> sees in this the germ of the Epic stories
<div n="lb"/>which are recorded in the Mahābhārata.
