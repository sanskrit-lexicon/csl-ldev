{@Loka@}¦ denotes ‘world’ in the Rigveda<sup>1</sup> and later.<sup>2</sup> Mention
is often made of the three worlds,<sup>3</sup> and {%ayaṃ lokaḥ,%} ‘this
world,’<sup>4</sup> is constantly opposed to {%asau lokaḥ,%}<sup>5</sup> ‘yonder world’
-{%i.e.,%} ‘heaven.’ Loka itself sometimes means ‘heaven,’<sup>6</sup>
while in other passages several different sorts of world are
mentioned.<sup>7</sup>
<F>1) Roth, St. Petersburg Dictionary,
{%s.v.%} 2, quotes no example of this mean-
ing for the Rigveda, where he sees the
word used only in the sense of ‘place,’
‘room,’ ‘free or open space.’ But Rv.
x. 14, 9, is a fairly certain example of
the wider sense.</F>
<F>2) Av. viii. 9, 1. 15; iv. 38, 5; xi. 5, 7;
8, 10, etc.; in ix. 5, 14, the worlds of
heaven ({%divya%}) and of earth ({%pārthiva%})
are distinguished; Vājasaneyi Saṃhitā,
xxxii. 11 {%et seq.,%} etc.</F>
<F>3) Av. x. 6, 31; xii. 3, 20; Aitareya
Brāhmaṇa, i. 5, 8; Satapatha Brāh-
maṇa, xiii. 1, 7, 3, etc.</F>
<F>4) Av. v. 30, 17; viii. 8, 8; xii. 5, 38;
xix. 54, 5; Vājasaneyi Saṃhitā, xix. 46,
etc.</F>
<F>5) Av. xii. 5, 38. 57; Taittirīya Saṃ-
hitā, i. 5, 9, 4; Aitareya Brāhmaṇa,
v. 28, 2; viii. 2, 3, etc.</F>
<F>6) Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa, ii. 6, 1, 7;
x. 5, 4, 16; xi. 2, 7, 19; and so probably
