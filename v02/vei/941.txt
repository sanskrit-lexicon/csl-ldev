{@Ghṛta,@}¦ the modern Ghee or ‘clarified butter,’ is repeatedly
<div n="lb"/>mentioned in the Rigveda<sup>1</sup> and later<sup>2</sup> both as in ordinary use
<div n="lb"/>and as a customary form of sacrifice. According to a citation
<div n="lb"/>in Sāyaṇa's commentary on the Aitareya Brāhmaṇa,<sup>3</sup> the dis-
<div n="lb"/>tinction between Ghṛta and {@Sarpis@} consisted in the latter being
<div n="lb"/>butter fully melted, while the former was butter melted and
<div n="lb"/>hardened ({%ghanī-bhūta%}), but this distinction cannot be pressed.
<div n="lb"/>Because the butter was thrown into the fire, Agni is styled
<div n="lb"/>‘butter-faced’ ({%ghṛta-pratīka%}),<sup>4</sup> ‘butter-backed’ ({%ghṛta-pṛṣṭha%}),<sup>5</sup>
<div n="lb"/>and ‘propitiated with butter’ ({%ghṛta-prasatta%}),<sup>6</sup> and ‘found of
<div n="lb"/>butter’ ({%ghṛta-prī%}).<sup>7</sup> Water was used to purify the butter: the
<div n="lb"/>waters were therefore called ‘butter-cleansing’ ({%ghṛta-pū%}).<sup>8</sup> In
<div n="lb"/>the Aitareya Brāhmaṇa^9 it is said that Ājya, Ghṛta, Āyuta, and
<div n="lb"/>Navanīta pertain to gods, men, Pitṛs, and embryos respectively.
