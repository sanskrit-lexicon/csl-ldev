{@Pañcāla@}¦ is the later name<sup>1</sup> of the people called {@Krivi@} in the
<div n="lb"/>Rigveda. The Pañcālas are rarely referred to except in con-
<div n="lb"/>nexion with the {@Kurus,@} and the kings of the Kuru-Pañcālas
<div n="lb"/>are mentioned in the Aitareya Brāhmaṇa.<sup>2</sup> In the Kāṭhaka
<div n="lb"/>Saṃhitā<sup>3</sup> the Pañcālas appear as the people of {@Keśin Dālbhya.@}
[Page1-469+ 46]
<div n="lb"/>In the Upaniṣads and later<sup>4</sup> the Brahmins of the Pañcālas
<div n="lb"/>figure as taking part in philosophical and philological dis-
<div n="lb"/>cussions. The Saṃhitopaniṣad Brāhmaṇa<sup>5</sup> makes mention of
<div n="lb"/>the Prācya-Pañcālas.
<div n="P"/>The Pañcālas, no doubt, included other tribes besides the
<div n="lb"/>Krivis. The name seems to refer to five tribes, and it has
<div n="lb"/>been suggested<sup>6</sup> that the Pañcālas represent the five tribes of
<div n="lb"/>the Rigveda, but the suggestion is not very probable. There
<div n="lb"/>is no trace in Vedic literature of the Epic division of the
<div n="lb"/>Pañcālas into northern ({%uttara%}) and southern ({%dakṣiṇa%}). The
<div n="lb"/>Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa<sup>7</sup> mentions their town {@Paricakrā;@} other
<div n="lb"/>towns to which allusion seems to be made were {@Kāmpīla@} and
<div n="lb"/>kauśāmbī.<sup>8</sup> Of their kings and chiefs, as distinguished from
<div n="lb"/>kings of the Kuru-Pañcālas, we hear of {@Kraivya, Durmukha,@}
<div n="lb"/>{@Pravāhaṇa Jaivali,@} and {@Śona.@}
