{@Hastin,@}¦ ‘having a hand,’ with {@Mṛga,@} ‘beast,’ denotes in the
<div n="lb"/>Rigveda<sup>1</sup> and the Atharvaveda<sup>2</sup> the ‘elephant.’ Later the
<div n="lb"/>adjective alone comes to mean ‘elephant.’<sup>3</sup> The animal was
<div n="lb"/>famed for its strength<sup>4</sup> as well as its virility.<sup>5</sup> It is mentioned
<div n="lb"/>with man and monkey as one of the beasts that take hold by
<div n="lb"/>the hand ({%hastādāna%}), as opposed to those that take hold by the
<div n="lb"/>mouth ({%mukhādāna%}).<sup>6</sup> It was tamed, as the expression {@Hastipa,@}
<div n="lb"/>‘elephant-keeper,’ shows, and tame elephants were used to
<div n="lb"/>catch others (see {@Vāraṇa@}). But there is no trace of its use in
<div n="lb"/>war, though Ktesias and Megasthenes both record such use for
[Page2-502+ 46]
<div n="lb"/>their times.<sup>7</sup> The Atharvaveda<sup>8</sup> alludes to its being pestered
<div n="lb"/>by mosquitoes.
