{@Gāthā@}¦ in the Rigveda<sup>1</sup> usually means only ‘song,’ ‘verse,’
<div n="lb"/>like Gātu.<sup>2</sup> In one passage,<sup>3</sup> however, it already has a more
<div n="lb"/>special sense, as it is classed with {@Nārāśaṃsī@} and {@Raibhī,@} a
<div n="lb"/>collocation repeatedly found later.<sup>4</sup> The commentators identify
<div n="lb"/>the three terms with certain verses of the Atharvaveda,<sup>5</sup> but
<div n="lb"/>Oldenberg<sup>6</sup> has shown that this identification is incorrect for
<div n="lb"/>the Rigveda. Gāthās are often mentioned elsewhere,<sup>7</sup> and are
<div n="lb"/>referred to as metrical in the Aitareya Āraṇyaka,<sup>8</sup> where the
<div n="lb"/>{@Ṛc, Kumbyā,@} and Gāthā are classed as forms of verse. The
<div n="lb"/>Aitareya Brāhmaṇa<sup>9</sup> distinguishes between Ṛc and Gāthā as
<div n="lb"/>divine and human respectively. According to the usage of the
[Page1-225+ 51]
<div n="lb"/>Brāhmaṇas and the liturgical literature, as stated by the St.
<div n="lb"/>Petersburg Dictionary, the Gāthās are, though religious in
<div n="lb"/>content, distinguished from Ṛc, Yajus, and Sāman as non-
<div n="lb"/>Vedic — that is, are not Mantras. This view is consistent with
<div n="lb"/>the fact that the phrase {@Yajña-gāthā,@} meaning a verse
<div n="lb"/>summarizing a sacrificial usage, is not rare. The Śatapatha
<div n="lb"/>Brāhmaṇa<sup>10</sup> preserves several Gāthās, which generally accord
<div n="lb"/>with this description as epitomizing the sacrifices of famous
<div n="lb"/>kings, and the Maitrāyaṇī Saṃhitā<sup>11</sup> states that a Gāthā is
<div n="lb"/>sung at a wedding. Sometimes<sup>12</sup> Gāthā is qualified as
<div n="lb"/>Nārāśaṃsī, where it must be a eulogy of a generous donor.
