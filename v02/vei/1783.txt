<L>1783<pc>1-518<k1>पारावत<k2>पारावत<h>2
2. {@Pārāvata@}¦ occurs in several passages of the Rigveda.
Roth<sup>1</sup> thinks that in most places<sup>2</sup> it means ‘coming from a
distance,’ but in two passages<sup>3</sup> he regards it as the proper
name of a people on the {@Yamunā@} (Jumna). It is certain that
in the Pañcaviṃśa Brāhmaṇa the Pārāvatas are a people on
that river ({%cf.%} {@Turaśravas@}). Hillebrandt<sup>4</sup> sees in all the
passages<sup>5</sup> the name of a people, comparing the <lang n="greek">Παρυῆται</lang> of
[Page1-519+ 52]
Ptolemy,<sup>6</sup> who apparently were settled on the northern border
of Gedrosia, or the <lang n="greek">Παροῦται</lang>, who were found in <lang n="greek">Αρεία</lang><sup>7</sup> He
suggests that they were originally ‘mountaineers’ ({%Cf.%} {@Parvata@}).
Ludwig<sup>8</sup> holds a similar view, and Geldner<sup>9</sup> recognizes a people
as meant. The mention of the {@Sarasvatī@} in connexion with
the Pārāvatas<sup>2</sup> in the Rigveda accords generally with their
position on the Yamunā in the Pañcaviṃśa Brāhmaṇa.<sup>3</sup>
