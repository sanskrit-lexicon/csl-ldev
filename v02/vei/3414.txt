<L>3414<pc>2-411<k1>संवत्सर<k2>संवत्सर
{@Saṃ-vatsara,@}¦ ‘year,’ is repeatedly mentioned from the
<div n="lb"/>Rigveda onwards.<sup>1</sup>
<div n="P"/>It duration was, according to the concurrent evidence of
<div n="lb"/>the Saṃhitās and Brāhmaṇas, 360 days, divided into 12 months,
<div n="lb"/>being, no doubt, roughly a lunar synodic year, which, however,
<div n="lb"/>it exceeded in length by 6 days.<sup>2</sup> As a solar year it appears
<div n="lb"/>only in the Nidāna Sūtra<sup>3</sup> of the Sāmaveda, where the sun is
<div n="lb"/>stated to spend 13(1/3) days in each of the 27 Nakṣatras.
[Page2-412+ 53]
<div n="P"/>The year being obviously out of harmony with the solar year
<div n="lb"/>(whether sidereal or tropical), efforts were certainly made to
<div n="lb"/>effect an assimilation of the natural and the accepted year. As
<div n="lb"/>has been seen (see {@Māsa@}), the evidence goes strongly to show
<div n="lb"/>that the intercalation was not an easy matter in the Brāh-
<div n="lb"/>maṇa period, though there are traces of what may be re-
<div n="lb"/>garded as a five-yearly or six-yearly intercalation. But there
<div n="lb"/>is no conclusive evidence that these periods were really
<div n="lb"/>observed.
<div n="P"/>Zimmer,<sup>4</sup> indeed, considers that the evidence required is
<div n="lb"/>afforded by the lists of the years, which are sometimes
<div n="lb"/>enumerated as five: Saṃvatsara, Parivatsara, Idāvatsara,
<div n="lb"/>Idvatsara, and Vatsara;<sup>5</sup> or Saṃvatsara, Parivatsara, Idāvat-
<div n="lb"/>sara, Iduvatsara, Vatsara;<sup>6</sup> or Saṃvatsara, Idāvatsara, Iduvat-
<div n="lb"/>sara, Idvatsara, Vatsara;<sup>7</sup> or Saṃvatsara, Parivatsara, Idāvat-
<div n="lb"/>sara, Anuvatsara, Udvatsara;<sup>8</sup> or Saṃvatsara, Parivatsara,
<div n="lb"/>Idāvatsara, Anuvatsara, Idvatsara.<sup>9</sup> But it must be noted not
<div n="lb"/>merely that the names vary considerably, but that four only are
<div n="lb"/>mentioned in some places,<sup>10</sup> in others<sup>11</sup> three, in others<sup>12</sup> two,
<div n="lb"/>and in yet others<sup>13</sup> six. Moreover, in none of these enumera-
<div n="lb"/>tions is there any reference to the names being connected with
<div n="lb"/>a system of intercalation. It is most probable that here we
<div n="lb"/>have no more that a mere series of priestly variations of
<div n="lb"/>Vatsara, based on the older and more genuine Saṃvatsara and
<div n="lb"/>Parivatsara as variants of the simple Vatsara, ‘year.’ The key
<div n="lb"/>to the invention of the series is probably to be found in
<div n="lb"/>passages like that of the Pañcaviṃśa Brāhmaṇa,<sup>14</sup> where the
<div n="lb"/>several {@Cāturmāsya@} (‘four-monthly’) sacrifices are equated
[Page2-413+ 54]
<div n="lb"/>with the different years.<sup>15</sup> Particularly unjustifiable is the
<div n="lb"/>attempt of Zimmer to see in the two-year series a series of two
<div n="lb"/>years of 354 days each, with an intercalary month in the
<div n="lb"/>second; for the year of 354 days, as such, is not known to have
<div n="lb"/>existed before the Sūtra period.
<div n="P"/>Zimmer<sup>16</sup> also finds an attempt at intercalation in the
<div n="lb"/>famous 12 days in which the Ṛbhus are said to have slept in
<div n="lb"/>the house of Agohya.<sup>17</sup> He thinks that they represent twelve
<div n="lb"/>days added at the winter solstice to equate the lunar year of
<div n="lb"/>354 days and the solar year of 366 days; and from the rever-
<div n="lb"/>ence paid in German antiquity to the ‘12 nights,’ he infers
<div n="lb"/>that this mode of intercalation is Indo-Germanic.<sup>18</sup> There can
<div n="lb"/>be little doubt that this view is wrong, and that the 12 days are
<div n="lb"/>merely the ‘reflexion of the year’ ({%saṃvatsarasya pratimā%})<sup>19</sup> in
<div n="lb"/>the sense that they represent the twelve months, and have no
<div n="lb"/>relation to chronology at all.
<div n="P"/>A reference to the use of Saṃvatsara alone as the fifth year
<div n="lb"/>of the cycle is seen by Shamasastry<sup>20</sup> in the peculiar dating of
<div n="lb"/>certain notices in the Baudhāyana Śrauta Sūtra,<sup>21</sup> but this view
<div n="lb"/>is improbable.<sup>22</sup>
