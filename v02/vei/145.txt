{@Arya.@}¦ — This word is not common in the older literature, in
<div n="lb"/>places where the quantity of the first vowel is fixed as short, except
<div n="lb"/>in a mere adjectival sense. Geldner,<sup>1</sup> indeed, contends that no
<div n="lb"/>other sense is anywhere needed; but Roth<sup>2</sup> and Zimmer<sup>3</sup> agree
<div n="lb"/>in thinking that in several passages of the Vājasaneyi Saṃhitā<sup>4</sup>
<div n="lb"/>the word has the same sense as {@Ārya,@} and this appears probable.
<div n="lb"/>Whether it is necessary to ascribe this sense to the word in the
<div n="lb"/>compound {%arya-patnī%}<sup>5</sup> applied to the waters set free by Indra,
<div n="lb"/>is more doubtful. The commentator, Mahīdhara,<sup>6</sup> suggests
<div n="lb"/>that the word means a {@Vaiśya,@} not an {@Ārya@} generally. This
<div n="lb"/>view is supported by the explanation in the Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa<sup>7</sup>
<div n="lb"/>of one of the passages of the Vājasaneyi Saṃhitā.<sup>8</sup> But though
<div n="lb"/>the use of Arya to denote a Vaiśya became common later, it is
<div n="lb"/>not clear that it was original.
