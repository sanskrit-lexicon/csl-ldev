{@Kṣetra,@}¦ ‘field.’ The use of this word in the Rigveda points
<div n="lb"/>clearly to the existence of separate fields<sup>1</sup> carefully measured
<div n="lb"/>off,<sup>2</sup> though in some passages the meaning is less definite,
[Page1-211+ 64]
<div n="lb"/>indicating cultivated land generally.<sup>3</sup> In the Atharvaveda<sup>4</sup> and
<div n="lb"/>later the sense of a separate field is clearly marked, though the
<div n="lb"/>more general use is also found.<sup>5</sup> The deity Kṣetrasya Pati,<sup>6</sup>
<div n="lb"/>‘Lord of the Field,’ should probably be understood as the god
<div n="lb"/>presiding over each field, just as Vāstoṣ Pati presides over each
<div n="lb"/>dwelling.<sup>7</sup> It is a fair conclusion from the evidence that the
<div n="lb"/>system of separate holdings already existed in early Vedic
<div n="lb"/>times.^8 See also {@Urvarā, Khilya.@}
