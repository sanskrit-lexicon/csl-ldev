{@Bhrātṛ@}¦ is the common designation of ‘brother’ from the
<div n="lb"/>Rigveda<sup>1</sup> onwards. The word is also applied to a relation or
<div n="lb"/>close friend generally,<sup>2</sup> but here the persons concerned are, it
<div n="lb"/>should be noted, in the Rigveda<sup>3</sup> deities, who are brothers of
<div n="lb"/>one another or of the worshipper. Thus in the early literature
<div n="lb"/>the word has not really lost its precise sense. The derivation
<div n="lb"/>from the root {%bhṛ,%} ‘support,’ is probably correct, designating
<div n="lb"/>the brother as the support of his sister. This harmonizes with
<div n="lb"/>the fact that in Vedic literature the brother plays the part of
<div n="lb"/>protector of his sister when bereft of her father, and that
<div n="lb"/>maidens deprived of their brothers ({%abhrātṛ%}) meet an evil fate.<sup>4</sup>
<div n="lb"/>The gradation of the relations in the home is shown by the
<div n="lb"/>order in the Chāndogya Upaniṣad,<sup>5</sup> where father, mother,
<div n="lb"/>brother, and sister are successively mentioned. Strife between
<div n="lb"/>brothers is occasionally referred to.<sup>6</sup>
