<L>2843<pc>2-274<k1>वसिष्ठ<k2>वसिष्ठ
{@Vasiṣṭha@}¦ is the name of one of the most prominent priestly
<div n="lb"/>figures of Vedic tradition. The seventh Maṇḍala of the
<div n="lb"/>Rigveda is ascribed to him; this ascription is borne out by the
<div n="lb"/>fact that the Vasiṣṭhas<sup>1</sup> and Vasiṣṭha<sup>2</sup> are frequently mentioned
<div n="lb"/>in that Maṇḍala, besides being sometimes referred to elsewhere.
<div n="lb"/>That by the name Vasiṣṭha a definite individual is always
<div n="lb"/>meant is most improbable, as Oldenberg<sup>3</sup> shows; Vasiṣṭha
<div n="lb"/>must normally mean simply ‘a Vasiṣṭha.’ But it is not
<div n="lb"/>necessary to deny that a real Vasiṣṭha existed, for one hymn<sup>4</sup>
<div n="lb"/>seems to show clear traces of his authorship, and of his assist-
<div n="lb"/>ance to {@Sudās@} against the ten kings.
<div n="P"/>The most important feature of Vasiṣṭha's life was apparently
[Page2-275+ 68]
<div n="lb"/>his hostility to {@Viśvāmitra.@} The latter was certainly<sup>5</sup> at one
<div n="lb"/>time the {@Purohita@} (‘domestic priest’) of Sudās, but he seems
<div n="lb"/>to have been deposed from that post, to have joined Sudās'
<div n="lb"/>enemies, and to have taken part in the onslaught of the kings
<div n="lb"/>against him, for the hymn of Sudās' triumph<sup>4</sup> has clear
<div n="lb"/>references to the ruin Viśvāmitra brought on his allies.<sup>6</sup>
<div n="lb"/>Oldenberg,<sup>7</sup> however, holds that the strife of Viśvāmitra and
<div n="lb"/>Vasiṣṭha is not to be found in the Rigveda. On the other
<div n="lb"/>hand, Geldner<sup>8</sup> is hardly right in finding in the Rigveda<sup>9</sup> a
<div n="lb"/>compressed account indicating the rivalry of {@Śakti,@} Vasiṣṭha's
<div n="lb"/>son, with Viśvāmitra, the acquisition by Viśvāmitra of special
<div n="lb"/>skill in speech, and the revenge of Viśvāmitra, who secured the
<div n="lb"/>death of Śakti by Sudās' servants, an account which is more
<div n="lb"/>fully related by Ṣaḍguruśiṣya,<sup>10</sup> which appeared in the Śāṭyāya-
<div n="lb"/>naka,<sup>11</sup> and to which reference seems to be made in the brief
<div n="lb"/>notices of the Taittirīya Saṃhitā<sup>12</sup> and the Pañcaviṃśa Brāh-
<div n="lb"/>maṇa<sup>13</sup> regarding Vasiṣṭha's sons having been slain, and his
<div n="lb"/>overcoming the Saudāsas. But it is important to note that no
<div n="lb"/>mention is made in these authorities of Sudās himself being
<div n="lb"/>actually opposed to Vasiṣṭha, while in the Aitareya Brāhmaṇa<sup>14</sup>
<div n="lb"/>Vasiṣṭha appears as the Purohita and consecrator of Sudās
<div n="lb"/>Paijavana. Yāska<sup>15</sup> recognizes Viśvāmitra as the Purohita of
<div n="lb"/>Sudās; this accords with what seems to have been the fact
[Page2-276+ 63]
<div n="lb"/>that Viśvāmitra originally held the post. Probably, however,
<div n="lb"/>with the disappearance of Sudās, Viśvāmitra recovered his
<div n="lb"/>position, whereupon Vasiṣṭha in revenge for the murder of
<div n="lb"/>his sons secured in some way unspecified the defeat of the
<div n="lb"/>Saudāsas.<sup>16</sup>
<div n="P"/>At any rate it is hardly necessary to suppose that the enmity
<div n="lb"/>of the Saudāsas and Vasiṣṭhas was permanent. There is
<div n="lb"/>evidence<sup>17</sup> that the Bharatas had the Vasiṣṭhas as Purohitas,
<div n="lb"/>while other versions<sup>18</sup> regard them as Purohitas for people
<div n="lb"/>({%prajāḥ%}) generally. It seems that the Vasiṣṭhas were pioneers
<div n="lb"/>in adopting the rule that Purohitas should act as Brahman
<div n="lb"/>priest<sup>19</sup> at the sacrifice: the Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa<sup>20</sup> states that
<div n="lb"/>the Vasiṣṭhas were once the only priests to act as Brahmans,
<div n="lb"/>but that later any priest could serve as such.<sup>21</sup> A rivalry
<div n="lb"/>with Jamadgni and Viśvāmitra is reported in the Taittirīya
<div n="lb"/>Saṃhitā.<sup>22</sup> Parāśara and Śatayātu are associated with Vasiṣṭha
<div n="lb"/>in the Rigveda,<sup>23</sup> being apparently, as Geldner<sup>24</sup> thinks, the
<div n="lb"/>grandson and a son of Vasiṣṭha. According to Pischel,<sup>25</sup> in
<div n="lb"/>another hymn,<sup>26</sup> Vasiṣṭha appears as attempting to steal the
<div n="lb"/>goods of his father Varuṇa; Geldner<sup>27</sup> also shows that the
<div n="lb"/>Rigveda<sup>28</sup> contains a clear reference to Vasiṣṭha's being a son
<div n="lb"/>of Varuṇa and the nymph Urvaśī. Perhaps this explains the
<div n="lb"/>fact that the Vasiṣṭhas are called the {@Tṛtsus@} in one passage
[Page2-277+ 52]
<div n="lb"/>of the Rigveda;<sup>29</sup> for being of miraculous parentage, Vasiṣṭha
<div n="lb"/>would need adoption into a Gotra, that of the princes whom
<div n="lb"/>he served, and to whom Agastya seems to have introduced
<div n="lb"/>him.
<div n="P"/>There are numerous other references to Vasiṣṭha as a Ṛṣi
<div n="lb"/>in Vedic literature,<sup>30</sup> in the Sūtras,<sup>31</sup> and in the Epic, where
<div n="lb"/>he and Viśvāmitra fight out their rivalry.<sup>32</sup>
