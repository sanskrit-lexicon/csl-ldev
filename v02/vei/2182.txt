{@Bṛbu@}¦ is mentioned in a hymn of the Rigveda,<sup>1</sup> where he is
<div n="lb"/>described as a most generous giver ({%sahasra-dātama%}), and as at
<div n="lb"/>the head of the {@Paṇis.@} According to the Śāṅkhāyana Śrauta
<div n="lb"/>Sūtra,<sup>2</sup> {@Bharadvāja@} received gifts from Bṛbu {@Takṣan@} and
[Page2-070+ 54]
<div n="lb"/>{@Prastoka Sārñjaya,@} a fact alluded to in the Mānava Dharma
<div n="lb"/>Śāstra,<sup>3</sup> where {%takṣan%} is treated as a descriptive attribute,
<div n="lb"/>‘a carpenter.’ Apparently Bṛbu was a Paṇi, though the words
<div n="lb"/>of the Rigveda<sup>1</sup> might be taken to mean that he was one who
<div n="lb"/>had overthrown them entirely. If so, Paṇi must here certainly
<div n="lb"/>mean a merchant in a good sense, Bṛbu being then a merchant
<div n="lb"/>prince.<sup>4</sup> According to Weber,<sup>5</sup> the name suggests connexion
<div n="lb"/>with Babylon, but this conjecture must be regarded as
<div n="lb"/>quite improbable. Hillebrandt<sup>6</sup> sensibly expresses no opinion
<div n="lb"/>as to Bṛbu, while Brunnhofer's<sup>7</sup> attempt to recognize a
<div n="lb"/>people named <lang n="greek">Τάσκοι</lang>, and to connect them with the
<div n="lb"/>Vedic word {%takṣan,%} is valueless, especially considering the
<div n="lb"/>fact that Takṣan is not found as an epithet of Bṛbu in the
<div n="lb"/>Rigveda.
