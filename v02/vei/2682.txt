{@Rājya@}¦ in the Atharvaveda<sup>1</sup> and later<sup>2</sup> regularly denotes
<div n="lb"/>‘sovereign power,’ from which, as the Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa<sup>3</sup>
<div n="lb"/>notes, the Brahmin is excluded.
<div n="P"/>In addition to Rājya, the texts give other expressions of
<div n="lb"/>sovereign power. Thus the Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa<sup>4</sup> contends
[Page2-221+ 43]
<div n="lb"/>that the Rājasūya sacrifice is that of a king, the {@Vājapeya@} that
<div n="lb"/>of a Samrāj or emperor, the status of the latter (Sāmrājya)
<div n="lb"/>being superior to that of the former (Rājya). The sitting on a
<div n="lb"/>throne ({@Āsandī@}) is given in the same text<sup>5</sup> as one of the
<div n="lb"/>characteristics of the Samrāj. Elsewhere<sup>6</sup> Svārājya, ‘uncon-
<div n="lb"/>trolled dominion,’ is opposed to Rājya. In the ritual of the
<div n="lb"/>{@Rājasūya@} the Aitareya Brāhmaṇa<sup>7</sup> gives a whole series of
<div n="lb"/>terms: Rājya, Sāmrājya, Bhaujya, Svārājya, Vairājya, Pāra-
<div n="lb"/>meṣṭhya, and Māhārājya, while Ādhipatya, ‘supreme power,’ is
<div n="lb"/>found elsewhere.<sup>8</sup> But there is no reason to believe that these
<div n="lb"/>terms refer to essentially different forms of authority. A king
<div n="lb"/>might be called a {@Mahārāja@} or a {@Samrāj,@} without really being
<div n="lb"/>an overlord of kings; he would be so termed if he were an
<div n="lb"/>important sovereign, or by his own entourage out of compli-
<div n="lb"/>ment, as was {@Janaka@} of {@Videha.@}<sup>9</sup> That a really great monarchy
<div n="lb"/>of the Aśoka or Gupta type ever existed in the Vedic period
<div n="lb"/>seems highly improbable.<sup>10</sup>
