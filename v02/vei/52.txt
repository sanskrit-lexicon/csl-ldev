{@Atharvāṅgirasaḥ.@}¦ — This is the collective name of the Athar-
<div n="lb"/>vaveda in several passages<sup>1</sup> of the later Brāhmaṇas. It occurs
<div n="lb"/>once in the Atharvaveda itself,<sup>2</sup> while the term Atharvaveda
<div n="lb"/>is not found before the Sūtra period.<sup>3</sup> The compound seems,
<div n="lb"/>according to Bloomfield,<sup>4</sup> to denote the two elements which
<div n="lb"/>make up the Atharvaveda. The former part refers to the aus-
<div n="lb"/>picious practices of the Veda ({%bheṣajāni%});<sup>5</sup> the latter to its hostile
<div n="lb"/>witchcraft, the {%yātu%}<sup>6</sup> or {%abhi-cāra.%}<sup>7</sup> This theory is supported by
<div n="lb"/>the names of the two mythic personages {@Ghora Āṅgirasa@} and
<div n="lb"/>{@Bhiṣaj Ātharvaṇa,@} as well as by the connection of Atharvāṇaḥ
<div n="lb"/>and {@Ātharvaṇāni@} with healing ({%bheṣaja%}) in the Pañcaviṃśa
<div n="lb"/>Brāhmaṇa.<sup>8</sup> Moreover, the term {%bhesajā%} (‘remedies’) designates
<div n="lb"/>in the Atharvaveda<sup>9</sup> that Veda itself, while in the Śatapatha
<div n="lb"/>Brāhmaṇa<sup>10</sup> {%yātu%} (‘sorcery’) conveys the same meaning. The
<div n="lb"/>evidence, however, being by no means convincing, it remains
<div n="lb"/>probable that there existed no clear differentiation between the
<div n="lb"/>two sages as responsible for the Atharvaveda as a whole.
